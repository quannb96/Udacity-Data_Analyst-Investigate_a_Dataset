Introduction

Dataset Description

This data set contains information about 10,000 movies collected from The Movie Database (TMDb), including user ratings and revenue. Certain columns, like cast and genres, contain multiple values separated by pipe (|) characters. There are some odd characters in the cast column. Dont worry about cleaning them. You can leave them as is. The final two columns ending with _adj show the budget and revenue of the associated movie in terms of 2010 dollars, accounting for inflation over time.

Question 1: How does the adjusted revenue of movies vary with their adjusted budget?

In[9]:
def plot_scatter(df, x_col, y_col, title):
    plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
    sns.scatterplot(x=x_col, y=y_col, data=df)
    plt.title(title)
    plt.xlabel(x_col.capitalize())  # Capitalize the column names for better visualization
    plt.ylabel(y_col.capitalize())
    plt.show()

# Question 1 : How does the adjusted revenue of movies vary with their adjusted budget?
plot_scatter(df, 'budget_adj', 'revenue_adj', 'Adjusted Revenue vs. Adjusted Budget')

The scatter plot revealed a positive correlation between budget and revenue, suggesting that movies with higher budgets tend to have higher revenue. However, there are also outliers where movies with relatively low budgets achieve high revenue, indicating that budget alone may not guarantee financial success.

##How do the popularity scores of movies vary across different release months?
# Extract release month from release_date
df['release_month'] = pd.to_datetime(df['release_date']).dt.month

# Calculate average popularity for each release month
monthly_avg_popularity = df.groupby('release_month')['popularity'].mean()

monthly_avg_popularity.plot(kind='bar', color='orange')

plt.xticks(rotation=0)

Movies released in May, June, July, November, and December tend to have higher average popularity scores. Conversely, films released in January, February, and September exhibit lower average popularity. This suggests that there may be seasonal trends in movie popularity, with certain months attracting more viewership than others.

Conclusions

In conclusion, our exploratory data analysis of the TMDb Movies dataset has provided valuable insights into various aspects of movie properties and their associations. Here are the key findings:

1: Properties Associated with High Revenues: We found that movies with higher budgets tend to generate higher revenues, indicating a positive correlation between budget and revenue. Additionally, popularity and vote average also showed positive correlations with revenue, suggesting that well-received movies tend to perform better financially.

2: Distribution of Adjusted Movie Revenue: The distribution of adjusted movie revenue is right-skewed, with a majority of movies having lower revenues and only a few outliers achieving significantly higher revenues.

3: Correlation between Adjusted Budget and Adjusted Revenue: Our analysis suggests a positive correlation between adjusted budget and adjusted revenue, indicating that higher budget movies tend to generate higher revenues. However, further statistical tests are needed to confirm this relationship.

4: Relationship between Movie Popularity and Adjusted Revenue: There appears to be a positive relationship between movie popularity and adjusted revenue, indicating that popular movies tend to have higher revenues.

5: Trend in Adjusted Revenue Over Release Years: The trend in adjusted revenue over release years shows variations, with some years having higher average revenues than others. This trend could be influenced by various factors such as economic conditions, industry trends, and audience preferences.

Limitations and Areas for Further Research

While our analysis provides valuable insights, there are some limitations to consider:

1: Causation vs. Correlation: It's important to note that our analysis primarily focused on exploring correlations between variables and does not imply causation. Further research using advanced statistical techniques could help identify causal relationships.

2: Data Quality: The dataset may contain errors or missing values that could affect the accuracy of our findings. Conducting a thorough data cleaning process and verifying the data quality could enhance the reliability of our analysis.

3: Sample Size and Representativeness: We are working with a sample of the overall movie population, and our findings may not generalize to the entire population. Additionally, the representativeness of our sample could be limited, leading to potential biases in our analysis.

4: Statistical Inference: Our analysis did not include statistical inference tests to assess the significance of our findings. Future research incorporating statistical tests could provide more robust evidence to support our conclusions.
